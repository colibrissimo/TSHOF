# Chapter Six: The Provost

The market square was different now.

Not visibly. The traders were trading. The hole was there, in the middle of everything, smelling of cedar. A. Octagon's pencil was in her pocket, the one that had been sharpened finer than any point had a right to be, and she had not told anyone about it, because some things needed to be held before they could be said.

But the square was different.

e.e. katoflio noticed it the way he noticed most things: by writing it down and then not quite being able to explain what he had written.

*Day 3 since A. Octagon came.*

*The hole: present. Cedar smell: confirmed. The square: changed.*

He looked at what he had written. He crossed out *changed* and wrote *occupied*. He crossed out *occupied* and wrote *known*. He left it at *known*, because that was the closest he could get, and being close was better than being wrong.

He was drawing a flower when the Provost arrived.


The Provost was a very large Dodecagon, and he moved the way very large Dodecagons moved: with the unhurried authority of someone who had never needed to hurry, because the world, in his experience, generally waited.

He did not look alarmed. He looked, if anything, faintly reassured, the expression of a shape who has heard about an irregularity and has come to establish that it is, in fact, regular.

"A. Octagon," he said.

"Provost," said A. Octagon.

He looked at the hole. He looked at e.e. katoflio. He looked at the seventeen flowers, most of which still had shadows, which was impossible, which he appeared to be filing carefully under *not yet explained*.

"I have had reports," he said, "of some irregular activity in the market square."

"Yes," said A. Octagon.

"I thought it best to see for myself." He paused. "There appear to be shadows."

"There do," said A. Octagon.

"Flowers don't have shadows," said the Provost, in the tone of someone who is not stating an opinion but reminding the world of a fact it seems to have temporarily mislaid.

"No," said A. Octagon. "They don't."

The Provost waited. He was very good at waiting, the patient waiting of someone who expects the world to correct itself, given enough time and reasonable management.

The shadows did not correct themselves.


e.e. katoflio looked at his notebook. He had a hypothesis about what was going to happen next, but he was eleven and his angles were still a work in progress, and he had learned, recently, that some experiments required you to watch rather than participate.

He watched.

"I understand," said the Provost, turning to A. Octagon with the careful courtesy of a shape who has excellent manners and intends to use them, "that you have been conducting observations. The Academy is, of course, interested in any rigorous study of the phenomenon." He paused. "What have you concluded?"

A. Octagon looked at the hole for a moment.

"I've concluded," she said, "that it responds to us trying."

The Provost nodded slowly. He had the expression of a person receiving information and fitting it, with some effort, into a frame it was not quite the right shape for.

"Responds," he said.

"Yes."

"And by *trying* you mean..."

"Drawing. Observing. Reaching toward it."

"I see." He said this in a way that suggested he did not see, but felt that *I see* was the appropriate response while he continued not seeing. "And this response, the shadows, you consider this a... a communication of some kind?"

"I consider it a beginning," said A. Octagon.

"A beginning," said the Provost. He tried the word as if testing its weight. "That is a large conclusion from a small observation."

"The observation is not small," said A. Octagon. "It is precise."

The Provost looked at her. He was looking at her the way he probably looked at official documents that contained something he was not yet prepared to act on.


"I must say," said the Provost, with great reasonableness, "that I am not entirely sure what we are protecting here. The hole is clearly an irregularity. Irregularities require documentation, study, possibly a committee. What they do not typically require is..." He gestured, carefully, at the flowers. "This."

"Experiment," said e.e. katoflio.

He had not meant to say it out loud.

The Provost looked at him. It was not an unfriendly look. It was the look of a very large Dodecagon acknowledging the existence of a small Isosceles with the patient tolerance of someone who has decided that children, while occasionally acute, belong to a different category of evidence.

"Children," said the Provost, "are often remarkably observant. That is one of their most useful qualities." He paused. "They are not always reliable in sequence."

"He has a notebook," said A. Octagon.

"Many children have notebooks."

"His has data."

e.e. katoflio kept his face still. He was getting better at this. He held his pencil and he did not look at the hole and he watched the Provost.

"I'm sure there is no cause," said the Provost, with great gentleness, "for irregular conclusions. We must be exact, A. Octagon. Excitement is not a measurement."

"I haven't measured my excitement," said A. Octagon. "I've measured the shadows."

A small silence.

"Seventeen," said e.e. katoflio.

"I beg your pardon?" said the Provost.

"Seventeen shadows. Each one corresponding to a drawn flower. The correlation is exact." He opened his notebook. "I have measurements."

The Provost looked at the notebook with the expression of someone who had been hoping not to be shown the measurements.

"Correlations," he said, "are not explanations."

"No," said e.e. katoflio. "But they're a start."


The Provost was quiet for a moment. He looked at the hole, which looked back (or didn't, it was difficult to say) smelling of cedar with its usual patience.

For just a moment, e.e. katoflio thought he saw it: a flicker of something in the Provost's face that was not reassurance and not authority and not the comfortable certainty that the world was ultimately orderly. Something that might have been, if you squinted and held very still, the beginning of a question.

Then the Provost straightened.

"I will," he said, "convene a preliminary review. The Academy will want to be involved." He looked at A. Octagon. "I trust you will document your observations through proper channels."

"I'll document them accurately," said A. Octagon.

"Through proper channels," said the Provost again, with emphasis.

"I will document them accurately," said A. Octagon again, with precision.

He turned to leave. He was three steps away when he paused.

He looked back at the hole.

"It does smell of cedar," he said, mostly to himself. Not quite a question. Not quite a statement.

He left before anyone could answer.
