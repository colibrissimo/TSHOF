# Chapter Two: Experiment Two

e.e. katoflio arrived at the market square before the traders did.

This was not unusual. What was unusual was that he arrived before he had decided why.

The seventeen flowers were still there. The shadows were still there. The one shadow that had leaned toward the hole was still leaning, as though it had made a decision and was not going to apologise for it.

e.e. katoflio looked at it for a long time.

Then he got out his notebook and wrote: *Experiment Two. Morning. Shadow: still leaning. Hypothesis: —*

He stopped.

He moved the flower that was casting the leaning shadow. Just slightly. A few lines to the left.

The shadow moved with it.

He moved it back.

The shadow moved back.

He moved a different flower — one of the ordinary ones, one whose shadow fell the correct way — closer to the hole.

Its shadow stayed ordinary.

e.e. katoflio sat very still.

He wrote: *Note. The response is not random.*

Then he crossed that out and wrote: *Note. Something is distinguishing.*

Then he crossed that out too and sat looking at the hole.

The hole looked back. Or it didn't. It was difficult to say. It smelled of cedar.


The thing was this.

If the response was not random — if something was distinguishing between the flowers, between positions, between *intentions*, maybe — then e.e. katoflio was not conducting an experiment in the usual sense.

He was being read.

He thought about A. Rhombus, who had explained on fourteen occasions that Flatland was complete and sufficient and did not require additional dimensions. He thought about what A. Rhombus would say about a shadow that leaned with intent.

He decided not to think about A. Rhombus.

He looked at his notebook instead. The flowers had been instruments. Good instruments. They had told him the hole was real, that it responded, that it distinguished. But flowers didn't say anything. Flowers were measurement.

If something on the other side was reading him, then measurement was no longer enough.

He needed to make a mark that meant something.

This felt both necessary and slightly embarrassing, the way that talking to yourself feels necessary and slightly embarrassing. He was eleven. He was a scientist. Scientists did not draw pictures for holes.

He opened his notebook to a clean page.

He drew an eye.


Just an eye. Not elaborate. Not decorated. The kind of eye you draw when you mean *I am looking at you* and you want that to be clear.

He tore it carefully from the notebook. He looked at it.

He looked at the hole.

He placed the drawing beside the hole with more care than he had given any of the flowers. He straightened it twice. He moved it one line closer. He moved it back.

In his notebook, he wrote: *Experiment Two. Object placed: one drawing. Subject: one eye. Purpose: —*

He stopped writing.

He looked at what he'd written. *Purpose* was not, strictly speaking, a scientific category. Purpose was what you had when you were trying to say something.

He left the line unfinished.

He stepped back.

The hole smelled of cedar. The leaning shadow leaned. A trader arrived at the far end of the square and began setting out angles, which was the sort of thing traders did in Flatland and which e.e. katoflio did not find interesting at all right now.

He waited.

Nothing happened yet.

e.e. katoflio drew something that wasn't a flower, and waited.
