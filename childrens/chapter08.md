# Chapter Eight: Under Review

The market square had acquired a rope.

It was a neat rope, properly knotted at each corner, strung between four small posts that had not been there the day before. Inside the rope: the hole, the seventeen flowers, and a measuring rod leaning against a post at a precise angle. Outside the rope: the traders, the square, the ordinary morning, and e.e. katoflio, who stood at the edge of what had been, until today, simply a place you could walk into.

He had his notebook. He did not open it.

A. Octagon was already there, standing outside the rope with the stillness of someone who has assessed a situation and not yet decided how to feel about it.

"When did this happen?" said e.e. katoflio.

"This morning," said A. Octagon. "Early."

He looked at the measuring rod. He looked at the flowers, which still had shadows, and which appeared not to have received any communication about the new arrangement. He looked at the hole, which smelled of cedar and looked like a hole, as it always did, and which was now inside a rope.

He wrote in his notebook: *Day 9. The square has been cordoned.*

He looked at this.

He added: *The flowers are still there.*

This seemed important to record.


The Academy functionary arrived at half past nine.

He was a slim Pentagon with a ledger under one arm and the expression of someone who finds the world most comfortable when it has been organised into columns. He introduced himself as a Preliminary Assessment Officer, which was a title that sounded as though it had been designed to prevent further questions.

"A. Octagon," he said, opening his ledger. "You are the registered observer of record for this irregularity."

"I have been observing," said A. Octagon.

"Yes." He made a note. "And your current classification of the phenomenon?"

A. Octagon looked at the hole.

"I don't have one," she said.

The functionary's pencil paused. "I beg your pardon?"

"I haven't classified it. I've been observing it. Those are different activities."

"Observation," said the functionary, with the patience of someone explaining something for what he hoped was the last time, "is the first stage of classification. We will need a preliminary designation in order to proceed with the review. Something provisional is quite acceptable." He consulted his ledger. "We have used *surface anomaly* in similar cases. Or *temporary irregularity pending further study.* Either would serve as a working description."

"Neither is accurate," said A. Octagon.

"They're provisional."

"They're inaccurate," said A. Octagon. "A surface anomaly is a mark or a crack. This is not a mark or a crack. A temporary irregularity suggests the irregularity is likely to resolve. I have no evidence that it will."


The functionary wrote something in his ledger with the careful deliberateness of a shape who is writing something down in order to move past the conversation it belongs to.

"Classification pending," he said. "That will have to do for now."

e.e. katoflio had been standing to one side, watching. He was getting better at this, at watching rather than participating. It was a useful skill and he did not entirely like it.

He watched the functionary measure the rope cordon. He watched him record the number of flowers. He watched him note, without expression, that the flowers had shadows, and write something in his ledger, and move on.

It was the moving on that stayed with e.e. katoflio. The functionary had looked at the seventeen shadows, which were impossible, which e.e. had spent weeks recording, and written something down and moved on. As though notation were the same as understanding. As though the ledger, once it contained the fact, had done what needed doing with it.


The Provost arrived later, when the functionary had finished and gone. He walked once around the cordon without speaking, looked at the measuring rod, looked at the hole.

e.e. katoflio watched him. The Provost did not look as comfortable as he had before.

"A. Octagon," said the Provost. "The review panel will meet on Thursday. I expect your observations submitted by Wednesday morning. Through proper channels."

"My observations will be accurate," said A. Octagon.

A small pause.

"Of course," said the Provost.

He left. He was almost at the edge of the square when he paused, briefly, in the way he had before, and then kept walking, and did not look back.


When the Provost was gone, and the functionary was gone, and the square had settled back into the ordinary business of trading, e.e. katoflio stood at the rope and looked at the measuring rod.

He had looked at it earlier. He had looked at it when the functionary measured the cordon, and when the Provost walked his circuit, and neither of them had looked at it properly, because it was their instrument and they had brought it here and it was therefore not interesting to them.

But e.e. katoflio had been looking at it all morning, in the way he looked at things now, which was slowly, and he had noticed.

The measuring rod had been sharpened.

Not damaged. Not worn. Sharpened. The way A. Octagon's pencil had been sharpened: finer than it had any right to be, more precise than it had been designed to be. The institution had placed its own instrument inside the cordon, and something on the other side had improved it.

He looked at A. Octagon.

She was already looking at the rod. She had seen it too. He did not know whether she had seen it before him or after, and it did not matter.

"It sharpened their measuring rod," he said.

"Yes," said A. Octagon.

"They won't notice."

A. Octagon looked at the rod for a moment longer.

"No," she said. "They won't."

e.e. katoflio wrote in his notebook: *The measuring rod has been sharpened. Nobody brought a sharpener.*

He looked at this. He did not know whether it was funny or frightening. He suspected it was both.


---


A. Octagon's rooms at the Registry were small and orderly and smelled of ink.

e.e. katoflio had come because he had told himself he was going to tell her, and then had walked there slowly, and then had stood at the door for longer than was strictly necessary. Now he was inside, watching her work.

She had several pages in front of her, ordered with the precision of someone who has spent a long time with evidence and knows how to treat it. She was holding her pencil, the sharpened pencil, and she was looking at the page rather than writing on it, choosing a word.

She had not appeared surprised to see him. She had simply said "Sit down," which he had done.

He sat. He watched her work. He thought about Tuesday.

The difficulty was that Tuesday, once spoken aloud, would no longer belong entirely to him. He had been carrying it in the private space between the notebook's last line and the next one.

A. Octagon looked up.

"You came back on Tuesday," she said. Not a question.

"Yes," said e.e. katoflio.

She waited. She was good at waiting; the quiet kind that simply made room.


e.e. katoflio looked at the window.

"I didn't set up flowers," he said. "I didn't try to repeat the method. I just had the drawing. The one of myself. I placed it near the hole." He paused. "Outside the rope."

A. Octagon said nothing. He understood this as permission to continue.

"The cedar smell didn't change first. It was the warmth. Near my hand. Not from the sun." He stopped. He had rehearsed this more than he had intended and was aware that rehearsal was not the same as accuracy. "It came to where I was. Not to the drawing. To me."

"How long did it stay?" said A. Octagon.

"I don't know. A while."

A. Octagon wrote something in the margin of the page.

"And then?" she said.

e.e. katoflio looked at the table.

"In the notebook," he said, "I wrote: *He came because he wanted to.*" He paused. "I crossed out *He* and wrote *It*, and then crossed that out and wrote *He* again."

A very small silence.

"Because *He* was accurate," said A. Octagon.

"Yes," said e.e. katoflio.


A. Octagon set down her pencil. She looked at the page in front of her.

"You understand," she said, "what that requires me to write."

It was not a question. But e.e. katoflio understood that it was something she needed to say aloud, in order to know whether she could bear to hear herself say it.

"Yes," he said.

She picked up her pencil. She was quiet for a long time.

"The accurate sentence," she said, mostly to herself, "is the one the review will find most difficult to hold." She paused. "That does not make it less accurate."

She began to write.

e.e. katoflio watched her. He could not see the exact words from where he was sitting, but he could see the quality of the writing: careful, unhurried, each word placed as though it were being measured before it was allowed to stay. The sharpened pencil moved across the page without scratching. Whatever she wrote, she wrote it properly.

He had known she would. That was not the question. The question had been whether he would be able to sit still and watch her carry the weight of it, and he found that he could.

She wrote for a while. Then she stopped.

The room smelled of ink. It had smelled of ink since he arrived; that was what the Registry smelled like. But underneath the ink, arriving so faintly that e.e. katoflio was not certain, for a moment, whether he was remembering it or sensing it, there was cedar.

Not the warmth. Not the presence. Just the smell, briefly, passing through the room the way a thought passes through a mind it does not belong to.

A. Octagon did not look up. She was inside the writing, and the writing was where she needed to be.

e.e. katoflio held still. The cedar arrived, and stayed for the length of a sentence, and left.

He did not write it down.


A. Octagon looked at what she had written.

"It will attract attention," said e.e. katoflio. He was not sure whether this was a question.

"Yes," said A. Octagon.

"Is there another way to write it?"

She looked at him then, the same look she had given him in the market square, the first time, after he had shown her the drawing. Not explanation. Not patience. Just recognition.

"There is always another way to write something," she said. "That is precisely the problem."

She gathered the pages together, aligned them with the same care she brought to measurements, and set them aside.

"Wednesday morning," she said. "Through proper channels."

e.e. katoflio stood. He looked at the pages on the desk. He could not read the observation from this angle, but he could see where the line he had told her had been written, and where her pencil had continued after.

He had come here with Tuesday inside him, entirely his. He was leaving without it.

He found, standing at the door of A. Octagon's rooms, that this was not the same as losing it.
