# Chapter Eight: Under Review

The market square had acquired a rope.

It was a neat rope, properly knotted at each corner, strung between four small posts that had not been there the day before. Inside the rope: the hole, the seventeen flowers, and a measuring rod leaning against a post at a precise angle. Outside the rope: the traders, the square, the ordinary morning, and e.e. katoflio, who stood at the edge of what had been, until today, simply a place you could walk into.

He had his notebook. He did not open it.

A. Octagon was already there, standing outside the rope with the particular stillness of someone who has assessed a situation and not yet decided how to feel about it.

"When did this happen?" said e.e. katoflio.

"This morning," said A. Octagon. "Early."

He looked at the measuring rod. He looked at the flowers, which still had shadows, and which appeared not to have received any communication about the new arrangement. He looked at the hole, which smelled of cedar and looked like a hole, as it always did, and which was now inside a rope.

He wrote in his notebook: *Day 9. The square has been cordoned.*

He looked at this.

He added: *The flowers are still there.*

This seemed important to record.



The Academy functionary arrived at half past nine.

He was a slim Pentagon with a ledger under one arm and the expression of someone who finds the world most comfortable when it has been organised into columns. He introduced himself as a Preliminary Assessment Officer, which was a title that sounded as though it had been designed to prevent further questions.

"A. Octagon," he said, opening his ledger. "You are the registered observer of record for this irregularity."

"I have been observing," said A. Octagon.

"Yes." He made a note. "And your current classification of the phenomenon?"

A. Octagon looked at the hole.

"I don't have one," she said.

The functionary's pencil paused. "I beg your pardon?"

"I haven't classified it. I've been observing it. Those are different activities."

"Observation," said the functionary, with the patience of someone explaining something for what he hoped was the last time, "is the first stage of classification. We will need a preliminary designation in order to proceed with the review. Something provisional is quite acceptable." He consulted his ledger. "We have used *surface anomaly* in similar cases. Or *temporary irregularity pending further study.* Either would serve as a working description."

A. Octagon was quiet for a moment.

"Neither is accurate," she said.

"They're provisional."

"They're inaccurate," said A. Octagon. "A surface anomaly is a mark or a crack. This is not a mark or a crack. A temporary irregularity suggests the irregularity is likely to resolve. I have no evidence that it will."


The functionary wrote something in his ledger with the careful deliberateness of a shape who is writing something down in order to move past the conversation it belongs to.

"Classification pending," he said. "That will have to do for now."

e.e. katoflio had been standing to one side, watching. He was getting better at this — at watching rather than participating. It was a useful skill and he did not entirely like it.

He watched the functionary measure the rope cordon. He watched him record the number of flowers. He watched him note, without expression, that the flowers had shadows, and write something in his ledger, and move on.

It was the moving on that stayed with e.e. katoflio. The functionary had looked at the seventeen shadows — which were impossible, which e.e. had spent weeks recording — and written something down and moved on. As though notation were the same as understanding. As though the ledger, once it contained the fact, had done what needed doing with it.

He thought about the drawing in his pocket. The small Isosceles. The cedar smell that had moved toward it on a Thursday.

That was not going in any ledger.



The Provost arrived later, when the functionary had finished and gone. He walked once around the cordon without speaking, looked at the measuring rod, looked at the hole.

e.e. katoflio watched him. The Provost did not look as comfortable as he had before.

"A. Octagon," said the Provost. "The review panel will meet on Thursday. I expect your observations submitted by Wednesday morning. Through proper channels."

"My observations will be accurate," said A. Octagon.

A small pause.

"Of course," said the Provost.

He left. He was almost at the edge of the square when he paused — briefly, in the way he had before — and then kept walking, and did not look back.



When he was gone, e.e. katoflio stood beside A. Octagon and looked at the cordon and the hole inside it.

"Can I still come here?" he said.

A. Octagon considered this with the seriousness it deserved.

"For now," she said. "But not inside the rope. And not alone." She paused. "Not officially."

e.e. katoflio looked at the rope. He looked at the hole, which was inside it, smelling of cedar, attending to whatever it attended to on the other side. He thought about Thursday — the review panel, the ledger, the language that made things smaller by naming them wrong.

He thought about the other Thursday. The drawing. The smell that had moved.

"Wednesday morning," he said. "That's when your observations are due."

"Yes," said A. Octagon.

"So Tuesday," said e.e. katoflio, "the square is still — "

"Tuesday," said A. Octagon carefully, "the review has not yet begun."

He nodded. He wrote in his notebook, not because he needed to but because writing things down was how he held them:

*Window: Tuesday.*

He closed the notebook and put it in his pocket, next to the drawing of a small Isosceles, roughly correct, with a notebook tucked under one angle.
