# Chapter Five: The Square

The market square was entirely ordinary.

This was, e.e. katoflio felt, slightly unfair. He had been carrying the drawing for two days. He had told A. Octagon. They had walked here together in the silence of two people who are about to do something that cannot be undone. The square should have acknowledged this in some way.

It did not. The traders were trading. A. Rhombus was visible at the far end, measuring something with an expression of great satisfaction. The hole was there, in the middle of everything, smelling of cedar and looking, as it always did, like a hole.

A. Octagon stood beside him and looked at it for a moment.

"Show me," she said. "Where the flowers were. Where you placed the drawing. Where you found it."

e.e. katoflio showed her. He indicated each position carefully, the way he had written it in his notebook: the row of flowers, the place beside the hole, the spot three lines away where the drawing had been lying face down. A. Octagon looked at each position in turn without rushing from one to the next.

"And the shadow that leaned," she said. "Which flower."

He showed her that too.

She nodded, as though this confirmed something she had not yet said aloud. Then she stood quietly beside the hole and looked at it, and e.e. katoflio stood beside her and looked at it, and nothing happened.


Nothing happened for quite a long time.

e.e. katoflio began to have the specific worry of someone who has told an adult about something impossible and is now watching the impossible fail to cooperate. He thought about the leaning shadow, which was not leaning at the moment. He thought about the returned drawing, which was in his pocket and which was undeniably real, but which was also just a drawing.

He thought about what it would mean to have brought A. Octagon here for nothing.

A. Octagon did not appear to be worried. She was simply present, in the way she was present for measurements: exactly there, not elsewhere, attending to what was in front of her. After a while she took a step closer to the hole.

Then another.

e.e. katoflio watched her.

She was not being dramatic about it. She was not announcing anything. She simply moved closer, the way you move closer when you want to see something properly, and then she lowered herself until she was at the edge of the hole, and she looked at it for a long moment, and then, as though the situation had a correct next step and she had identified it, she placed her palm flat against the edge.

Not pushing through. Not reaching for anything. Just present.

e.e. katoflio felt something he had not expected: fear. Not of the hole. Of what might happen to her, now that he had brought her here.

He said nothing. He did not pull her back.


The cedar smell grew closer.

Not stronger, exactly. Closer. As though something on the other side had noticed the palm and moved toward it.

And then e.e. katoflio saw something at the edge of the hole, something small and careful, at the limit of what the light allowed. And his breath stopped in a way that had nothing to do with fear.

A. Octagon's face changed.

She was looking at the hole. But she had felt something. e.e. katoflio could tell from the way she became very still, and from the way she did not pull her hand back.

They stayed like that for a moment.

Then A. Octagon lifted her palm from the edge. She looked at it. She looked at the hole. The cedar smell settled back to its usual distance.

She was quiet for a long time.

Then she reached behind her side and took out her pencil.

She looked at it.

The pencil had been sharpened. Not by her. The point was finer than any point had a right to be. She had written three pages this morning and it had not dulled at all.


She held it for a moment. Then she held it out to e.e. katoflio.

He took it. He looked at the point. He understood.

"It was here," he said. "Before. In this square. It sharpened things."

"Yes," said A. Octagon.

"I didn't know it had been here."

"No," she said. "Neither did I."

She looked back at the hole. The hole looked back, or it didn't (it was a hole, and it was still difficult to say) and it smelled of cedar, which was the only answer it ever gave and which was, e.e. katoflio was beginning to understand, enough.

He gave the pencil back to her.

"It's careful," he said. He was not sure where the word came from. It felt correct.

A. Octagon looked at the pencil. Then she looked at him with an expression he had not seen on an adult's face before: not disbelief, not explanation, not the particular patience adults used when they were about to tell you something was not quite right.

Just recognition.

"Yes," she said. "I think it is."

They stood there in the ordinary market square, in the cedar smell, with the sharpened pencil between them, and neither of them said anything else, because there was nothing else that needed saying yet.
