# Chapter Four: A. Octagon

e.e. katoflio had not slept well.

This was not because of the hole, exactly. It was because of the drawing, which he had put on his desk before going to bed, and which had been there when he woke up, and which was still more eye than he had made it, and which he had wrapped carefully in a page from the back of his notebook before putting it in his pocket, because it seemed wrong to carry it loose.

He needed to tell someone.

Not his mother, who was a respectable Scalene and would ask sensible questions he did not have sensible answers to. Not A. Rhombus, for reasons that did not require explanation. Not any of the other children, who would either not believe him or believe him too loudly and ruin everything.

He needed someone who noticed things before deciding what they were.

He thought about this on his way to the market square, and the answer arrived, as answers sometimes do, before he had finished asking the question properly.

A. Octagon.


She was outside the Registry, which was where she usually was in the mornings, going over the week's measurements with the kind of attention that made other people feel their own measurements were slightly approximate. She had eight sides and a pencil behind one of them, and she was not, e.e. katoflio had always noticed, the sort of person who looked up before she was ready to.

He waited.

She looked up.

"e.e. katoflio," she said, which was exactly what she always said, and which somehow always sounded like she had been expecting him specifically.

He opened his mouth to explain. What came out was: "There's a hole in the market square."

A. Octagon looked at him. "I know," she said. "Several people have reported it."

"It smells of cedar."

"Yes."

"And I drew —" He stopped. He had been going to explain the flowers, and the shadows, and the seventeen, and the one that leaned, and then the eye and the waiting and the nothing and then finding it three lines away, but that was not the right order. None of that was the point.

He put his hand in his pocket.

"I drew something," he said, "and it came back."


A. Octagon was quiet for a moment. Not the quiet of someone deciding whether to believe him. The quiet of someone making room.

"May I see?" she said.

He took the wrapped drawing from his pocket. He held it for just a moment — one moment, which he did not examine too closely — and then handed it to her.

She unwrapped it carefully. She held it at the proper angle. She looked at it for a long time without saying what it was or wasn't.

"You drew this," she said.

"Yes."

"And it came back like this."

"Yes. It was three lines away. Face down."

She turned it slightly. Just slightly, the way you turn something when precision matters.

"The line here," she said, indicating the upper left, where the wobble had been. "Was that yours?"

e.e. katoflio looked. "The wobble was mine," he said. "The rest of it —"

"Yes," said A. Octagon.

She looked at it a moment longer. Then she wrapped it again, exactly as he had wrapped it, and held it out to him.

"Will you take me there?" she said.


It was not a dramatic question. She said it the way she said everything: as though the situation had a correct next step and she was identifying it.

e.e. katoflio took the drawing back. It was still his. That was — he was relieved. He was also aware of something else, smaller and less comfortable, which was that it would not be only his for very much longer.

"Now?" he said.

"If that suits," said A. Octagon. She put the pencil back behind her side, closed the Registry ledger, and looked at him with the expression she used when the measurements were finished and the real work was about to begin.

e.e. katoflio put the drawing back in his pocket.

They walked toward the market square together, which was not something he had imagined, and which felt, in a way he could not have explained to anyone, like the correct next step.
