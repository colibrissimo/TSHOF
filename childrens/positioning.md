# One-page positioning note (perplexity)
## Positioning

This project appears to occupy a credible gap in the current children’s market: An illustrated chapter book for ages 7–12. In a flat world where everything has exactly two dimensions and everything makes sense, a child named e.e. katoflio starts drawing flowers next to a hole that smells of cedar — and something on the other side notices. A story about curiosity, first contact, and a hedgehog who was always passing through until someone gave him a reason to stay.

The concept is strongest when framed as **emotion-led adventure with geometric worldbuilding** rather than as a children’s retelling of a Victorian satire.  That distinction matters because current children’s publishing does support chapter books and highly illustrated fiction as a bridge to independent reading, but the market language is built around readability, humour, wonder, and heart. [carolinewakeman](https://carolinewakeman.com)

## Market gap

The defensible claim is **not** that there are no children’s books involving maths or shapes.  The sharper claim is that there is little visible evidence of a **UK/US trade illustrated chapter book** for 7–12s that combines named characters, dimensional travel, emotional stakes, and the conceptual inheritance of *Flatland*. [books.apple](https://books.apple.com/us/book/flatland-the-movie-edition/id652829288)

That gives the project a useful in-between position: not a worksheet-like geometry book, not an adult mathematical fantasia, and not generic portal fantasy.  It potentially fills a space between educational appeal and genuine trade storytelling. [luckylittlelearners](https://luckylittlelearners.com/childrens-books-teach-geometry/)

## Risks

The main commercial risk is **over-intellectual positioning**.  If the pitch sounds like a lesson in topology or a respectful homage to Abbott, it may read as niche; if it foregrounds friendship, exclusion, curiosity, bravery, and the shock of discovering another dimension, it becomes much easier to place in the trade market. [penguin.co](https://www.penguin.co.uk/children/7-9-years)

A second risk is format confusion.  You will help yourself if the project is clearly presented as either an **illustrated chapter book** for developing independent readers or a **middle-grade illustrated novel**, rather than something floating between picture book, novelty book, and educational tie-in. [carolinewakeman](https://carolinewakeman.com)

## Agent/editor pitch

A strong short pitch could be:

**An illustrated chapter book for ages 7–12, set in a rule-bound two-dimensional world where a child protagonist discovers that reality is larger, stranger, and more frightening than anyone has been taught to believe. Combining visual wit, emotional stakes, and dimensional adventure, it offers a child-friendly entry point into the imaginative territory opened by *Flatland* without requiring any prior knowledge of Abbott.** [search.worldcat](https://search.worldcat.org/title/Flatland/oclc/1142753209)

A slightly punchier version for a covering letter:

**This is a highly illustrated, emotionally driven chapter book that brings the wonder of dimensional discovery to young readers in a format the market already knows how to sell, while occupying a space that appears under-served by existing UK/US trade titles.** [readbrightly](https://www.readbrightly.com/illustrated-chapter-book-series-early-readers/)

## UK-facing targets

For UK submission strategy, the broadest fit is likely agents and agencies already working across children’s fiction, middle grade, and visually distinctive projects.  The search results point to agencies such as A.M. Heath, David Higham, United Agents, Darley Anderson Children’s, and The Agency as examples of established UK players with children’s lists. [reedsy](https://reedsy.com/resources/literary-agents/gb/childrens-book/)

Be critical in targeting, though: this is not for every children’s agent.  It is likely best suited to agents who can sell **concept-rich commercial middle grade**, **illustrated fiction**, or **genre-leaning children’s books**, rather than agents focused mainly on preschool picture books or straight realism. [unitedagents.co](https://www.unitedagents.co.uk/childrens-authors-and-illustrators)
