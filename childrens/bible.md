1. Odilon in one sentence: Odilon is a small hedgehog-like being covered in sharpened coloured pencils who notices edges, improves what he touches, thinks a great deal about lunch, and slowly discovers that staying can matter more than passing through. => all four pillars of the character:
* visual hook — hedgehog + pencils
* story function — he changes things by sharpening / refining
* comic rhythm — lunch
* heart — he learns presence, not just passage 
1. What Odilon is in story terms Odilon is not just “the cute one.” ^^ Odilon is:
* the mystery
* the magic system
* the geometry mascot
* the emotional hinge
* the proof that another way of seeing is possible (and WE would know)

⠀For the younger version, that means he should feel like a blend of:
* unexpected classroom character
* gentle inter-dimensional visitor
* visual embodiment of points / lines / angles / colour
* slightly odd friend who communicates through actions before words (that's my synaesthesia talking, you might want to push back hard here)

⠀He does not need to explain himself much. In fact, he is stronger if he remains partly mysterious. 
1. The core truth of Odilon If we strip away all the beautiful "grown-up" atmosphere and ask what children will feel from him, I think it is this: Odilon is careful. Not flashy. Not chaotic. Not a prank machine. Not a manic mascot. He is careful with:
* pencils
* drawings
* edges
* contact
* attention
* other people’s trying => think of when A. Octagon’s palm and Odilon’s nose is so moving: it is not spectacle, it is care returned.

1. Odilon’s “want” and “need” For the story's architecture? What Odilon wants At first, nothing very emotional. He wants to:
* move through
* sharpen what needs sharpening
* continue onward
* think about lunch He is a creature of habit and motion. What Odilon needs He needs to discover:
* that being noticed changes you
* that staying is a kind of shape
* that attention can be mutual
* that care can create a home We shouldn't make Odilon too chatty or too self-aware too early. More in the relation through repeated contact.

1. Odilon’s sensory signature This where we stay in the book, us, in the atmosphere. Odilon should always carry a sensory world:
* smell: cedar, graphite, warm wood shavings
* sound: tiny clicks, soft scritches, faint pencil-taps
* texture: polished wood, sharp points, small warm nose
* colour: rainbow gradation, but orderly rather than random
* movement: careful padding, slight bristling, subtle pauses The writing should rely more on concrete sensory recurrence than abstract concepts. Not: “the ontology of passage” But: “the square smelled of cedar before Odilon arrived.” Not: “recognition across dimensions” But: “the drawing came back, and the eyes looked as though they had been looking.” => The returned drawing with changed eyes: strong child-facing ideas

1. Odilon’s rules: what he should not do Avoid making Odilon:
* a chatterbox
* an explainer
* a fully verbal guide
* chaotic magic
* a wish-granter
* omnipotent He is stronger if he remains:
* specific
* consistent
* partly mysterious
* emotionally legible through behaviour, not speeches If he ever “speaks,” I would do it very sparingly: perhaps not with ordinary dialogue at all?

1. Odilon’s emotional arc across Part or I should say Book One? If we make him central-but-mysterious, his arc becomes beautifully simple. Stage 1: Passing through (this could be a joke too: imagine other characters in a hurry: "Just passing through"?, ugh dunno) He does not mean to make contact. He sharpens, then moves on. Stage 2:  Noticing He realises someone is trying to communicate.  Stage 3: Returning He comes back on purpose. The returned drawing is a key turning point. Stage 4: Staying He chooses presence over motion. That is his deepest act. For children, that arc reads as: “The creature became our friend.” For adults, it still holds all the richer thematic meaning we built underneath?
2. Odilon’s story function in a children rewrite To consider: The children version is not “What is a multidimensional being?” but rather “Can the child and Odilon learn how to say hello?” => over simplification? And if we make that shift, a lot becomes clear?
* the child protagonist moves to the centre
* experiments matter
* drawings matter
* the first contact matters more than the Academy’s commentary
* the returned drawing becomes a major emotional hinge
* the ending becomes: he stayed
1. The phrases worth keeping around Odilon Coined phrases that are too good to lose, but they should be used more sparingly and more like anchors with children. Keepable recurring language we built:
* smelled of cedar
* patient as weather
* Here is the shape of me 
* thinking about lunch
* it responds to us trying Those can become refrain-like phrases a child recognises and anticipates.
1. The one danger to avoid with Odilon The danger is making him too symbolic too early. He absolutely can carry symbolism, but children must first encounter him as:
* creature
* mystery
* friend
* pattern-maker
* careful responder Meaning comes later?
